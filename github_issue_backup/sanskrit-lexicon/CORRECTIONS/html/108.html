PD headwords with different Devanagari and IAST spellings

funderburkjim commented on 2015-05-11 01:48:20

There is a certain duplication of headword spelling that is present in PD.
For nearly every headword, the headword is given in two forms, Devanagari and IAST.

In the Cologne digitization, these two forms were coded in different ways. The Devanagari was coded in HK (which we have by now changed to SLP1), and the IAST was coded with the 'Anglicized Sanskrit' method which represents IAST diacritics with numbers following the letter. Here is how the digitizations look for the three headwords of the above image:

<P>.{#aMhohan#}¦ (am3ho-han)
<P>.{#aMhohi#}¦ (am3hohi) 
<P>.{#aMhi#}¦ (am3h-ri)

Now further imagine converting the IAST to SLP1 - we expect to get THE SAME SPELLING as the coding of the Devanagari form of the headword.

This is the case with the first one (we ignore the '-' in am3ho-han and use the fact that 'm3' is a representation of anusvAra). Similarly, when the IAST of the second example is converted to SLP1, we get the same spelling as the SLP1 coding of Devanagari.

But look at the third example - Here, the SLP1 transcoding of the IAST is aMhri, which differs from the SLP1 coding aMhi of the Devanagari --- something is wrong. Brief examination (say of the scan) for this case shows that the aMhi spelling is wrong.

Now a relatively simple program was used to carry out this analysis for all headwords. The analysis results in about 1500 (out of 107,620 headwords) cases where the Devanagari and IAST spellings are inconsistent.

I think this list deserves further examination, as it will likely uncover many spelling errors - either in the Devanagari coding or in the IAST coding.

After the corrections under issue 106, there are 1427 cases where the Devanagari and IAST spellings are inconsistent. A first presentation of these is in file pd_deva_ne_iast.txt.

Note1: the first 4 cases can be ignored - the difference is due to IAST ambiguity in handling the hiatus 'ai'. Note2: 4756 systematic IAST/DEVA differences ignored.

Because 1427 is a large number of cases, we need to develop some ways to expedite the analysis of these cases.
Based on examination of 80 of the cases in connection with issue#106, I expect most of the errors to be in the coding of Devanagari, and most of these are due to typographical errors. However, a small percentage (5% ?) will likely be due to errors in the text. And a similarly small percentage will be errors in the printed IAST.
